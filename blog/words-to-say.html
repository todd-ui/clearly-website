The One Word to Drop (and the One Word to Embrace) for Healthier Co-Parenting

Co-parenting after separation can feel like navigating a minefield. You're trying to do what's best for your kids while managing your own emotions and the complex dynamics with your ex-partner. Most advice focuses on communication strategies, boundaries, and scheduling. But there's something more fundamental that can transform your co-parenting relationship: changing just two words in your vocabulary.

The Word to Eliminate: "Fair"

Stop trying to make co-parenting "fair." This might sound counterintuitive, but hear me out.

When parents get caught up in what's "fair," they create an invisible scorecard. She got them for the holiday last year, so it's my turn. He only pays for school supplies, so I shouldn't have to cover sports fees. I did drop-off three times this week, so they owe me.

This fairness trap turns co-parenting into a transaction—a constant tallying of who gave what, who sacrificed more, who's pulling their weight. It breeds resentment, not cooperation. You start making decisions based on evening the score rather than what actually serves your children.

Here's the truth: co-parenting will never be perfectly fair. One parent might have more financial resources. The other might have more flexible work hours. One household might be closer to school. These imbalances are just realities, not injustices to be corrected.

When you're focused on fairness, you're looking backward—at what was given or taken. You're keeping score. And kids can sense that energy, even when you think you're hiding it.

The Word to Embrace: "Flexible"

Replace "fair" with "flexible," and watch how the dynamic shifts.

Flexibility means responding to what's actually happening in the moment rather than adhering rigidly to what the custody agreement says or what feels balanced on paper. It means saying yes when your ex asks to switch weekends because their parent is in town, even if it's inconvenient for you. It means covering an unexpected expense without immediately calculating whether it's "their turn" to pay.

This doesn't mean being a doormat. Flexibility requires boundaries—clear ones. But those boundaries are based on your actual capacity and your children's needs, not on abstract notions of equity.

When you approach co-parenting with flexibility, you're modeling something powerful for your kids: that relationships can be collaborative rather than adversarial, that people can adapt to serve something bigger than themselves, and that love isn't a zero-sum game.

Why This Shift Matters

Children of divorce don't need perfect equality between households. They need parents who can work together without visible tension. They need to see adults who can compromise, who prioritize the collective good over individual wins.

When you stop asking "Is this fair?" and start asking "Is this flexible enough to meet everyone's needs right now?"—that's when co-parenting becomes less exhausting. You're no longer expending energy on mental accounting. You're simply responding to the situation in front of you.

Some days you'll give more. Some days you'll receive more. Over time, it might not balance perfectly, and that's okay. What matters is that your children grow up in an environment where cooperation, not competition, defines their parents' relationship.

Putting It Into Practice

Start small. The next time you feel that familiar pang of "that's not fair," pause. Take a breath. Ask yourself: What's the flexible response here? What serves my child in this moment?

You might be surprised how liberating it feels to stop keeping score.
